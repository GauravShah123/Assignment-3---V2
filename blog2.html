Notes from 'How to Win Friends and Influence People'

  • Product Management
  • Social
  • Photo by Melanie Stander on Unsplash

I was recently recommended the book “How to Win Friends and Influence People” by Dale Carnegie. I can’t explain how much this book shifted my perspective on relationships and communication. Here is a quick list of everything I learnt.

Part 1 - Introduction

Criticism is futile

  • Positive reinforcement is more effective than criticism.
  • Understand others' justifications and calmly explain yours.
  • Wrongdoers often believe they are right.

Appreciation and Praise

  • Be generous with praise and sparing with criticism.
  • Be sincere and avoid flattery.
  • Notice and point out people's good qualities.

What they want matters

  • Appeal to what others care about, not just what you care about.
  • When requesting or negotiating, consider what would make them want to agree.
  • Help them envision the positive results of the change you propose.

Enthusiasm is key

  • People mirror the emotions you show them.
  • Display energy and enthusiasm to evoke the same response in others.

Part 2 - Being more likeable

Be more interested in other people

  • Show thoughtfulness without being performative.
  • Remember important dates like birthdays and reach out.
  • Take an interest in their passions and learn from them.

Smile

  • A smile conveys happiness and leaves a positive impression.
  • It brings happiness to others and improves their perception of you.

Remember first names

  • Names are important to people and make them feel valued.
  • Mispronouncing or misspelling names can have a negative effect.

Be an attentive listener

  • Encourage others to talk about themselves.
  • Show genuine interest and ask enjoyable questions.
  • Especially effective when dealing with angry customers.

Talk in terms of their interests

  • Learn about topics others are passionate about.
  • Engage in discussions, even if it's not your own passion.
  • Show energy and enthusiasm during conversations.

Be nice

  • Display common courtesy and politeness.

Part 3 - How to influence people

You can't win an argument

  • Avoid arguments to maintain goodwill.
  • Agree with others to prevent unnecessary conflict.
  • Help others feel a sense of importance.

Step 1: Welcome the disagreement

  • Disagreement is valuable for growth and improvement.

Step 2: Disregard your first instinct

  • Avoid getting defensive or angry during disagreements.

Step 3: Listen first

  • Listening shows respect and can prevent mistakes.
  • Disagreement often stems from a passion for the topic.

Step 4: Postpone

  • Allow time for reflection before continuing the discussion.
  • Consider the merit and importance of the disagreement.

Step 5: Disarm

  • Avoid announcing the intention to change others' minds.
  • Phrase statements in a way that encourages joint examination of facts.

Never say "you're wrong"

  • Directly stating someone is wrong diminishes their self-esteem.

Admit if you are wrong (quickly and emphatically)

  • Apologize before being confronted.
  • Admitting mistakes disarms others and increases forgiveness.

Get them saying yes at the start

  • Start with points of agreement to establish rapport.
  • Priming others to say "yes" makes further agreement more likely.

Let people think it was their idea

  • Ask others for their input to make them feel involved.
  • Help them arrive at your desired outcome without resistance.

Think from their perspective

  • Consider why others hold their beliefs.
  • Show how your perspective benefits them.

Appeal to their noble motives

  • Recognize and appreciate the noble aspects of others.
  • Make them aware of their positive qualities to gain compliance.

Dramatize your ideas

  • Use vivid language and creativity to make conversations engaging.
  • Incorporate gamification or friendly competition when appropriate.

Part 4 - Being a (not bossy) Leader

Giving feedback

  • Begin with positive feedback before addressing criticisms.
  • Point out specific strengths and areas for improvement.

How to criticize

  • Be subtle in delivering criticism.
  • Suggest improvements while acknowledging existing skills.

Negative but Positive

  • Place positive remarks before negative ones.
  • Emphasize the positive aspects and encourage improvement.

Talk about your mistakes before criticizing others

  • Humanize yourself and avoid appearing superior.
  • Show that the goal is to improve the work, not belittle others.

Ask questions, don't give orders

  • Use phrases like "Maybe..." or "Consider doing..."
  • Encourage brainstorming and involvement in decision-making.

Let people save face

  • Reprimand in a way that minimizes humiliation.
  • Give notice, explain reasons politely, and express confidence in them.

Praise

  • Explain why and how someone's work is commendable.
  • Provide specific and meaningful feedback.

Give a dog a good name

  • Assume positive traits in others and explain their positive reputation.
  • Motivate others to live up to their perceived reputation.

Make the person happy to do what you suggest

  • Be sincere and focus on the other person's benefit.
  • Clearly communicate your desired action.
  • Show empathy and understanding.
  • Highlight how following your suggestion helps them.
  • Align the benefits to their wants and needs.
  • Explain the personal benefits they can gain.

Conclusion

Apart from a great deal of learnings, this book also left me with a lot of questions. As I was reading, I made sure to note them down to answer in future blog articles.
